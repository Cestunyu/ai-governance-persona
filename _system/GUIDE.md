# AI Social Thought Spectrum Guide

This guide defines how to study AI social thought without turning it into a flat list of opinions.

## What Counts As Understanding

For each text, you should be able to explain:

- the central claim
- the threat or opportunity model
- the implied model of human agency
- the implied model of AI agency
- the preferred institutional response
- the strongest opposing position
- why the text became influential

## Classification Rules

Do not classify by tone alone.

- A text can be pessimistic about current AI and still not be doomerist.
- A text can be optimistic about AI and still be human-centered.
- A text can be anti-humanist without being technically serious.
- A technical text can carry social thought by changing what kind of human agency seems plausible.

## Recommended Sequence

1. Map extremes first: Yudkowsky, Sutton, Andreessen, Land.
2. Add central safety philosophy: Bostrom, Russell, Christiano.
3. Add critical AI: Bender/Gebru/Mitchell/McMillan-Major, Crawford, Benjamin.
4. Add institutional frontier-lab positions: Anthropic, OpenAI.
5. Add normal-technology skepticism: Narayanan/Kapoor, Marcus, Mitchell.
6. Add genealogy: cybernetics, Extropianism, singularity, accelerationism.

## Failure Modes

- Treating `AI safety` as one position.
- Treating `optimism` as one position.
- Treating `Bitter Lesson` as a complete political theory.
- Treating `critical AI` as merely anti-AI.
- Overweighting fringe accelerationism because it is rhetorically vivid.
- Underweighting institutional texts because they feel less philosophical.
