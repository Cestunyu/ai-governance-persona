AI Stance Map

Not Every “AI Risk” or “AI Optimism” Claim Means the Same Thing

This map places major AI thinkers, institutions, and movements on one shared field. It asks three questions: what they think the main problem is, how they want AI governed, and where humans should stand in the future of intelligence. The two-dimensional map below is a reading tool, not a permanent label for anyone.

“Worried about AI” is not one position Yudkowsky worries about loss of control and extinction risk; Bender/Gebru about data, labor, environment, and power; Narayanan/Kapoor about overclaiming and misuse.
“Pro-AI” is not one position either Andrew Ng emphasizes productivity, Altman governed AGI, Kurzweil the singularity, and e/acc acceleration. These are not the same optimism.
Technical beliefs change political instincts If you believe scale and search will keep producing breakthroughs, your instincts about regulation, open source, and deployment speed will differ from someone who sees today’s AI as overclaimed.

How to read the map

Reference Points The thinkers, institutions, and movements on the map are comparison points. They do not determine your quiz result; they help explain who a region resembles and which positions sit nearby or opposite.

Upper left is not generic pessimism

It usually combines civilization-level risk judgment, strong governance position, and a human-protection goal: Yudkowsky, Russell, and Christiano sit around this region.

The top is not one kind of humanism

Critical AI, Pope Leo XIV, and Russell all sit high, but for different reasons: social justice, human dignity, and human-compatible control are different value languages.

The right side is not one optimism

Andrew Ng emphasizes applications and productivity; Kurzweil emphasizes singularity and human-machine merger; e/acc emphasizes open acceleration. They are all rightward, but not vertically alike.

Capability belief is separate

Someone can think today’s AI is overclaimed and still support open deployment; someone can believe AI is very powerful and still demand strict governance. Do not collapse capability belief into the coordinate.

How this relates to the quiz

This map explains the classification frame behind the quiz. The quiz itself is closed-ended: the core coordinates come only from agreement items, while scenario choices and final self-placement are used as cross-checks to avoid confusing nearby positions.

1. Core scored items

Nine five-point agreement items cover Risk Judgment, Governance Position, and Value Position. They produce the two-dimensional coordinate and spectrum label.

2. Scenario cross-checks

Four concrete scenario items do not move the coordinate, but provide weak evidence about how a user reacts in release, public-sector, research, and open-source contexts.

3. Final self-placement

The final multiple-choice item does not change the coordinate. It helps distinguish nearby types and lets users check whether the result feels recognizable.

4. Reference points

The thinkers and movements on the scatterplot do not determine your result. They are reference points for understanding who a position resembles, who sits nearby, and who sits opposite.

Why not just ask “Are you afraid of AI?”

Because some people fear loss of control, some fear corporate and state power, some fear overclaiming, and some fear too much restriction. One question would collapse these positions together.

Why show capability belief?

Capability belief is not a fourth coordinate. It is a secondary tag: is the user closer to transformative AI, mixed evidence, or bubble skepticism?

Profile vs map

The quiz gives a short profile. The map shows finer-grained thinkers and movements. The profile is shareable; the map is for understanding the underlying differences.

This is not a psychometric scale

It is a public reading and self-placement tool. Coordinates help comparison; they do not imply stable personality types in a psychometric sense.

Method and limitations

This is an interpretive map, not an academic scale. Each point is hand-coded from public texts, institutional documents, and long-running public positions, then compressed from three judgment dimensions into a two-dimensional coordinate for easier comparison.

What is being compared

The map includes people, institutions, and movements. It compares the dominant tendency in public arguments and representative texts; it is not a final summary of anyone’s entire thought.

How the horizontal axis works

The horizontal axis combines the quiz's Governance Position and Risk Judgment: more control, pause, and civilization-risk judgment moves left; more deployment, openness, and opportunity-cost concern moves right.

How the vertical axis works

The vertical axis corresponds to the quiz's Value Position: upward means more emphasis on human dignity, public accountability, democracy, and social structures; downward means more emphasis on intelligence, computation, techno-capital, or posthuman directions.

Uncertainty

Complex thinkers span multiple positions. New texts, events, or better evidence should change coordinates. This page presents the current version of the reading frame.

Main regions

People who sit near each other do not necessarily agree. They simply share a center of gravity across the three questions. The region notes below are a reading guide.

Upper left: catastrophic risk, control, and human protection

This region gathers civilization-level risk, AI alignment, and strong-governance positions. They usually see the risk as high, governance as needing caution, and the goal as protecting humanity.

  • Yudkowsky / MIRI
  • Bostrom
  • Russell, Christiano, Cotra, Hendrycks

Upper middle: social harm, power, and ordinary technology

This region does not necessarily accept the AGI extinction story. It puts AI back into institutions, data, labor, race, environment, and overclaiming, with strong emphasis on human society and public accountability.

  • Bender / Gebru / Mitchell / McMillan-Major
  • Crawford, Benjamin
  • Narayanan / Kapoor, Marcus / Mitchell

Upper right: building, deployment, and human benefit

This region acknowledges AI governance problems but thinks applications, productivity, and scientific gains should move forward. It stresses the opportunity cost of underdeployment and frames value around human benefit.

  • Andrew Ng
  • Sam Altman / OpenAI
  • Dario Amodei / Anthropic sits between safety and building

Lower left: strong system governance and posthuman control

This region distrusts open diffusion but also does not treat current human preferences as the final measure. It emphasizes planetary-scale computation, system architecture, and non-traditional human-centered governance.

  • Benjamin Bratton
  • Controlled Posthumanism / Posthuman Control

Lower right: scale, open acceleration, and posthuman boundaries

This region moves from technical epistemology into open acceleration, singularity, or techno-capital narratives. Sutton anchors scale and general methods through the Bitter Lesson; e/acc and Land move toward open acceleration and strong posthuman boundaries.

  • Rich Sutton
  • Ray Kurzweil, Marc Andreessen
  • e/acc, Nick Land

Middle right: architecture optimism and world models

This region rejects near-term doom narratives and supports openness and building, but is not simply “bigger language models are enough” optimism. The core claim is that world models, planning, and new architectures matter.

  • Yann LeCun
  • Architecture optimism / World models

Suggested reading path

If the goal is to build judgment quickly, do not read randomly by name. Start with anchors for each region, then return to specific thinkers.

Step 1: technical epistemology and the safety extreme

Start with Sutton’s The Bitter Lesson, then read Yudkowsky 2008/2022 and Bostrom 2012/2014. This explains why scale is attractive and why some people treat loss of control as an extinction problem.

Step 2: architecture optimism and ordinary technology

Read LeCun’s A Path Towards Autonomous Machine Intelligence, then Marcus/Mitchell and Narayanan/Kapoor. This separates “today’s large-model route is not enough” from “AI is overclaimed.”

Step 3: critical AI

Read Stochastic Parrots, Crawford, Benjamin, and Narayanan/Kapoor. They pull the debate from future AGI back to institutions, data, labor, race, environment, and evaluation.

Step 4: optimism and accelerationism

Read Amodei, OpenAI, Ng, Andreessen, Kurzweil, e/acc, and Land. The point is not only who is right, but how each defines benefit, constraint, humanity, and the future.
