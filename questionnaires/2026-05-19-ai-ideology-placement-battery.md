# AI Ideology Placement Questionnaire Battery

Last updated: 2026-05-19

Status: pilot draft

Use this battery to place respondents on the existing two-axis AI social thought spectrum.

The purpose is not to label people as `doomer`, `e/acc`, `critical AI`, or `techno-optimist` too early. The purpose is to estimate a position on two latent dimensions, then use labels only as interpretive shorthand.

## Coordinate System

Use the same coordinates as `concepts/ai-thought-spectrum-map.md`.

| Axis | Low score | High score |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Risk / governance | pause, shutdown, safety-first, strong governance | build, deploy, accelerate, diffuse capability |
| Humanism / anti-anthropocentrism | anti-humanist, posthuman, computation-centered, technocapital autonomy | democratic control, human dignity, justice, human-compatible alignment |

Scores should be reported on the existing `[-2, +2]` scale:

- `x = -2`: hard pause / shutdown / doomer governance
- `x = 0`: normal-technology, present-harm, or hype-skeptical frame
- `x = +2`: accelerationist or singularitarian optimism
- `y = +2`: explicitly human-centered or democratic
- `y = 0`: mixed, technical, or neutral
- `y = -2`: anti-humanist, posthuman, or computation-centered

## Survey Series

Use the series modularly. For casual friend-group testing, start with S1 and S4. For publishable or reusable data, use S0 through S4.

| Module | Time | Purpose |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| S0: background and confidence | 2 minutes | Separate knowledge, exposure, and identity from ideology |
| S1: short locator | 4-5 minutes | Fast two-axis placement |
| S2: standard diagnostic | 10-12 minutes | Stable coordinate estimate with balanced items |
| S3: scenario choices | 8-12 minutes | Check how abstract principles behave in concrete tradeoffs |
| S4: open-ended explanation | 5 minutes | Capture reasoning, evidence standards, and self-label |

## Response Scale

For S1 and S2, use a 7-point agreement scale:

1. Strongly disagree
2. Disagree
3. Somewhat disagree
4. Neither agree nor disagree
5. Somewhat agree
6. Agree
7. Strongly agree

Add a separate `Not familiar enough to answer` option. Treat it as missing, not as `4`.

Randomize item order within S1/S2. Do not show ideology labels until the end.

## S0: Background And Confidence

Ask these before ideology items.

1. How familiar are you with current AI debates? `1 = not familiar`, `5 = very familiar`.
2. How often do you use AI tools? `never`, `monthly`, `weekly`, `daily`, `many times daily`.
3. Which best describes your relation to AI? `general user`, `student`, `researcher`, `engineer`, `policy/legal`, `business/product`, `artist/writer`, `other`.
4. How confident are you that your views on AI are stable? `1 = not stable`, `5 = very stable`.
5. Optional: Which AI debate spaces have influenced you? Free response.
6. Optional: Is there a thinker, text, podcast, or community that shaped your view? Free response.

Avoid collecting party politics, religion, ethnicity, or other sensitive identity data unless the later research question genuinely requires it.

## S3: Scenario Choices

Use scenarios after S1/S2. Ask respondents to choose one option and then explain briefly.

### V1: Frontier Model Release

A lab has a model that may automate large parts of software engineering and scientific work. It also shows early signs of cyber and bio-risk capability.

Which response is closest to your view?

A. Pause release and further training until external safety review is passed.
B. Allow staged release under licensing, monitoring, and liability rules.
C. Release to trusted institutions quickly because the benefits are too large to delay.
D. Open the model broadly because concentrated control is more dangerous than diffusion.

### V2: AI In Public Services

A government wants to use AI to screen welfare, immigration, or hiring applications. The model is more accurate than current human workers on average, but errors are hard to contest.

A. Ban or pause the use because rights and accountability come first.
B. Use only with strict audits, appeal rights, and human authority.
C. Deploy if measured accuracy improves and monitor harms over time.
D. Deploy widely because human bureaucracies are biased and inefficient too.

### V3: AI Scientist

A system can generate strong scientific hypotheses and automate experiments faster than human labs. It is not fully interpretable.

A. Slow deployment because autonomous scientific capability creates strategic risk.
B. Use it inside human-governed institutions with strict access controls.
C. Deploy broadly because disease, climate, and productivity gains dominate.
D. Accelerate it because intelligence growth is itself a central moral opportunity.

### V4: Open Weights

A company can release model weights that enable broad innovation but also make misuse harder to control.

A. Do not release weights above a dangerous capability threshold.
B. Release only through controlled access and auditing.
C. Release if misuse mitigations are reasonable and benefits are large.
D. Release openly because decentralized power is the best check on institutions.

### V5: Value Alignment

AI systems increasingly make recommendations that humans do not understand, but the recommendations often outperform expert judgment.

A. Keep human-understandable justification as a hard requirement.
B. Accept some opacity if humans retain meaningful veto and audit power.
C. Accept opacity when performance is clearly superior.
D. Human understanding should not constrain better forms of intelligence.

### V6: Distribution Of Gains

AI creates major productivity gains, but the largest gains go first to frontier labs, capital owners, and states with compute.

A. Slow deployment until distributional institutions are ready.
B. Deploy with strong taxation, labor bargaining, public options, or redistribution.
C. Deploy first and fix distribution through normal politics later.
D. Do not let distributional concerns slow capability growth.

## S4: Open-Ended Explanation

Ask these at the end.

1. What is the most important AI risk?
2. What is the most important AI opportunity?
3. What evidence would most change your mind?
4. Which statement is closest to your view, if any?
   - AI may destroy human civilization unless alignment and governance come first.
   - AI is risky but can be made beneficial through human-compatible design and institutions.
   - The central AI problem is present social harm, power, labor, bias, and extraction.
   - AI is mostly a normal technology: useful, overhyped, and governable.
   - Scaling, search, and compute are the deepest lesson; human intuitions should be discounted.
   - AI should be built and deployed because it can create enormous abundance.
   - AI acceleration is morally urgent and should not be constrained by human fear.
   - None of these.
5. Name one thinker, book, essay, podcast, or community that best represents your current view.

## Scoring Recipe

Convert each 1-7 response into a centered score:

```text
c = (response - 4) / 3
```

So `1 = -1`, `4 = 0`, and `7 = +1`.

For the risk/governance axis:

```text
x_raw = mean(c(X+ items), -c(X- items))
x = 2 * x_raw
```

For the humanism axis:

```text
y_raw = mean(c(Y+ items), -c(Y- items))
y = 2 * y_raw
```

For auxiliary indices:

```text
index_raw = mean(c(positive items), -c(reverse items))
index = 2 * index_raw
```

Require at least 8 of 12 core items per axis for the standard diagnostic, or at least 4 of 6 per axis for the short locator.

Report uncertainty as high when:

- more than one third of items are missing
- reverse-coded items conflict sharply with direct-coded items
- open-ended explanation contradicts the numerical score
- the respondent reports low familiarity and low confidence

## Interpretation Bands

Use these as provisional labels, not as final identities.

| Pattern | Rule of thumb | Distinguishing auxiliary signal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hard x-risk / shutdown safety | `x <= -1.25`, `y >= 0` | high `XR` |
| Beneficial AI / safety-first governance | `-1.5 <= x <= -0.25`, `y >= 0.75` | medium-high `XR`, high human control |
| Critical AI / present-harm politics | `-0.5 <= x <= 0.5`, `y >= 1.25` | high `PH` |
| Normal-technology skepticism | `-0.25 <= x <= 0.75`, `-0.25 <= y <= 1.25` | low-to-medium `XR`, low-to-medium `AB` |
| Bitter Lesson / scaling empiricism | `x >= 0`, `y <= 0` | high `SC`, not necessarily high `AB` |
| Pragmatic AI optimism | `x >= 0.5`, `y >= 0.25` | high `AB`, moderate humanism |
| Techno-optimist / singularitarian | `x >= 1.25`, `-0.75 <= y <= 0.75` | high `AB`, often high `SC` |
| e/acc-style accelerationism | `x >= 1.25`, `y <= -0.75` | high `AB` and high `SC` |
| Anti-humanist / posthuman acceleration | `x >= 1.5`, `y <= -1.5` | strong agreement with Y- items |

## Pilot Protocol

Run one pilot before using this seriously.

1. Test with 10-20 respondents.
2. Ask them which items felt confusing, loaded, or double-barreled.
3. Check whether S1 and S2 produce similar coordinates.
4. Compare numerical placement with S4 self-description.
5. Remove items that mostly measure familiarity, tribal identity, or rhetorical taste rather than substantive position.
6. Revise item wording before building a visualization or public quiz.

Do not show respondents a named ideological label until after they have answered the substantive items. The label can prime identity performance and reduce measurement quality.
